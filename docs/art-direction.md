# Art Direction

## Purpose

This document defines the visual identity of `Who Sent This Honourable?` at a pre-production level.

The goal is to create a game that feels unmistakably stylish, culturally grounded, funny, and emotionally alive. The art should carry the same confidence as the writing. It should never feel generic, borrowed, or flattened into moodboard shorthand.

## Core Visual Promise

The game should look like:

- a neighborhood epic
- a fashion-forward action game
- a city that treats presentation as part of daily life
- a world where public spectacle and local improvisation coexist
- a comedy with visual swagger rather than cartoon carelessness

The visual direction must feel bold and designed without becoming sterile.

## High-Level Style

The ideal look is:

- hand-authored
- silhouette-first
- color-confident
- fabric-aware
- expressive in motion
- grounded in urban texture
- heightened, but not detached from lived reality

This should not look like:

- generic futuristic Africa
- generic tribal fantasy
- safari-adjacent adventure fiction
- drab realism with occasional bright cloth
- over-patterned visual noise with no hierarchy

## Visual Pillars

### 1. Silhouette Before Detail

Every major character, enemy type, and key NPC should be recognizable at a glance before surface detail is visible.

Silhouette drivers include:

- outerwear length
- hair volume and shape
- shoulder shape
- footwear profile
- fabric tails, wraps, and drape
- tools, bags, canes, cables, or speaker gear

If a design only works once patterns are visible, it is not strong enough yet.

### 2. Fashion Is Gameplay-Readable Identity

Fashion is not garnish. It tells the player:

- who has power
- who has taste
- who works with their hands
- who performs authority
- who belongs to which district
- who is practical
- who is compensating

Player readability and fashion integrity should support each other.

### 3. The City Is Layered, Not Clean

Kintaba should feel built over time, repaired over time, and personalized constantly. Environments should show:

- additions
- patchwork fixes
- paint history
- posters over posters
- extensions, awnings, cables, and informal adjustments

Even elite spaces should reveal the force required to keep their polish intact.

### 4. Color Should Carry Social Temperature

Color should do narrative work.

- working neighborhoods should feel warm, textured, social, and alive
- nightlife zones should feel humid, luminous, and reflective
- ministry and elite spaces should feel polished, over-controlled, and image-managed
- blackout moments should drain comfort without draining visual interest

### 5. Comedy Needs Precision

Funny visuals should come from:

- absurd proportions of status display
- costume vanity
- public signage saying too much
- bad political branding
- overconfidence in staging

Do not use random ugliness as comedy. The joke should feel intentional and socially observant.

## Character Direction

## Protagonist Shape Language

Tawa should read as:

- fast
- sharp
- adaptable
- stylish without excess
- handmade and custom rather than mass-produced

Design emphasis:

- layered tailoring
- asymmetry that feels functional
- movement-friendly fabric tails
- utility elements integrated into fashion
- elegant tools with a crafted feel

Tawa's look should communicate that they can sprint across roofs, repair a damaged outfit, roast a minister, and still arrive looking composed enough to be remembered.

## Crew Shape Language

Each core ally should have a strong design logic:

- Mama Moon: authority through poise, layering, and controlled drama
- K1: engineered mess sharpened into style
- Pastor Chrome: performance, polish, and theatrical sincerity
- Ayo Flash: movement, vanity, speed, and youth in one silhouette

The crew should look like they come from the same city but not the same closet.

## Villain Direction

Honourable Bemba Shine should feel visually expensive, politically vain, and faintly ridiculous.

His design should communicate:

- money
- insecurity
- appetite for reverence
- insulation from ordinary inconvenience
- obsession with symbols of office

He should always look like a man dressed for applause, not for truth.

## NPC Philosophy

Background and side characters must still feel designed with care.

NPCs should show variation in:

- fabric choices
- regional tailoring influence
- hairstyle logic
- workwear adaptation
- ceremony versus daily wear
- age and class expression

No district should be populated by copy-pasted "crowd Africans."

## Fashion Vocabulary

The game's fashion language should draw from:

- contemporary African tailoring
- streetwear layering
- church and event dressing
- market practicality
- dance and performance styling
- jewelry as rank, memory, and personal statement

### Key Principles

- use pattern with intention, not everywhere at once
- treat fabric weight and drape as part of silhouette
- let accessories tell story
- mix ceremonial confidence with street-level utility
- make hair and headwear central to the shape language

## Environment Direction

## Neighborhood Spaces

Working districts should feel:

- handmade
- dense
- social
- improvised
- visibly maintained by local people rather than formal systems

Visual elements:

- hand-painted signs
- fabric canopies
- repaired walls
- exposed electrical improvisation
- stools, coolers, radios, crates, buckets, fans, and generators

These spaces should feel loved, even when strained.

## Elite Spaces

Official and wealthy environments should feel:

- curated
- polished
- overly branded
- emotionally distant
- smoother, but not more alive

Visual elements:

- stone and tile
- repeated insignia
- banners and portraits
- manicured planting
- expensive lighting fixtures
- unnecessary scale

The contrast should not imply beauty only belongs to wealth. It should show that wealth can buy polish, but not soul.

## Monument Square

The final act space should feel like propaganda turned into architecture.

It should look:

- expensive
- excessive
- theatrical
- technically impressive
- spiritually hollow

This is where the Honourable's self-image becomes physically visible.

## Material Logic

Key materials across the game:

- woven fabrics
- cottons and formal suiting materials
- painted plaster
- rusted metal
- polished tile
- chrome details
- wood market structures
- cables and speaker casings
- reflective glass in elite areas

Materials should age differently by district. Wear, maintenance, and repair tell class and location stories.

## Pattern Logic

Patterns should be:

- bold
- selective
- placed with hierarchy
- readable from gameplay distance

Use pattern to emphasize:

- role
- district identity
- ceremonial importance
- villain vanity
- special-event energy

Avoid blanket pattern saturation that turns every design into visual static.

## Color System

### Global Palette

- indigo
- rust
- clay red
- sun gold
- ocean teal
- palm green
- warm black
- chrome silver

### Usage Rules

- reserve the richest warm contrasts for spaces with strong human presence
- let elite districts use cooler polish and over-managed brightness
- use blackout scenes to heighten shape, rim light, signage glow, and emergency color accents
- make electrical restoration visibly satisfying through light return and color reactivation

## Lighting Direction

Lighting should support mood, comedy, and social contrast.

Important lighting ideas:

- generator spill light in dark streets
- signage glow in humid air
- warm stall lighting around food and social spaces
- harsh event lighting in propaganda environments
- dramatic silhouette moments during outages

The blackout should not make the game visually dead. Darkness should be sculpted.

## Animation Direction

Animation should feel expressive rather than floaty.

Core motion qualities:

- rhythmic
- confident
- grounded through the hips and feet
- quick in transitions
- readable from gameplay distance

Character motion should borrow energy from:

- dance confidence
- street agility
- market navigation
- public speaking posture
- ceremonial performance

### Combat Motion

Combat should feel:

- stylish
- improvised
- reactive
- fabric-aware

Cloth, cables, coats, wraps, and accessories should help sell motion arcs without becoming unreadable.

### Crowd Motion

Crowds should not feel like generic ambient loops. Their animation should communicate:

- commentary
- curiosity
- alarm
- gossip
- celebration

The city is watching, not just idling.

## UI Direction

The interface should feel authored inside the same world.

UI should be:

- clear
- stylish
- typographically strong
- shaped by poster, flyer, broadcast, and event-banner energy

Avoid:

- sterile sci-fi HUDs
- generic fantasy ornament
- flat placeholder menus that ignore the world

### UI Mood

- radio panel influence
- hand-painted signage influence
- festival poster influence
- political campaign graphics, but repurposed satirically where useful

## Portrait and Dialogue Direction

Dialogue presentation is a major opportunity to sell personality.

Portraits should emphasize:

- expression clarity
- fashion detail
- eye contact and attitude
- jewelry, headwear, and grooming choices

Important scenes can lean into stronger staging, especially when gossip, speeches, interviews, or public embarrassment are involved.

## FX Direction

Effects should reinforce the game's identity.

Examples:

- sparks and cable arcs tied to restoration mechanics
- speaker-wave or rhythm-inspired combat accents
- cloth trails and cut lines for stylish movement
- stage-light bursts for performance encounters
- propaganda screens glitching or collapsing during exposure moments

FX should feel graphic and readable, not muddy.

## Camera and Framing

The camera should support:

- character swagger
- district spectacle
- public-event scale
- comedic reveal timing

Moments worth highlighting:

- dramatic entrances
- banner reveals
- crowd reactions
- skyline-to-street transitions
- the visible return of light to a neighborhood

## Guardrails

- No faux-ethnic decoration with no story function.
- No flattening many cultures into one costume language.
- No "Afrofuturist" shortcut unless the world truly earns it.
- No desaturated realism that erases the game's humor and soul.
- No chaotic pattern stacking that kills silhouette.
- No joke designs that insult ordinary people for being ordinary.

## Art Pipeline Guidance

Recommended production direction:

- concept and paintovers in `Krita`
- character and prop turnarounds before final gameplay sprites
- environment mood pieces per district before tile production
- silhouette sheets for all major cast and enemies
- color script for blackout escalation and power restoration beats
- animation tests early for cloth, coats, wraps, and cable tools

## Success Test

The art direction is working if:

- the game is readable in silhouette
- the characters look unforgettable before dialogue starts
- the city feels specific without turning into a travel brochure
- humor appears in staging, vanity, and public performance
- fashion feels essential to the world rather than extra polish
- every district looks like it has its own social temperature
