# Audio Direction

## Purpose

This document defines the sound identity of `Who Sent This Honourable?` at a pre-production level.

The audio should not function as generic polish added after gameplay. Sound is one of the main ways the city becomes believable, funny, warm, political, and emotionally alive.

## Core Audio Promise

The game should sound like:

- a city that is always in conversation with itself
- a place where music is public life, not background flavor
- a blackout that removes more than light
- a comedy carried by rhythm, timing, tone, and interruption
- a world where generators, radios, voices, traffic, cooking, and ceremony all matter

The player should be able to close their eyes and still feel where they are.

## Audio Pillars

### 1. The City Is Never Sonically Empty

Kintaba should sound alive even during quiet moments.

Ambient sound should suggest:

- people nearby
- business in progress
- arguments half-heard
- music leaking from somewhere unseen
- tools, transport, and daily work
- distance between public noise and private intimacy

Silence should be used carefully, because in this world silence means something is wrong.

### 2. Music Is Social Infrastructure

Music in this game is not just emotion support for the player. It is how districts express themselves, how events gather momentum, how memory travels, and how electricity becomes audible.

When music disappears because of the blackout, the player should feel the loss immediately.

### 3. Comedy Lives in Timing and Delivery

Audio comedy should come from:

- dead-serious line delivery
- public announcements that sound polished and useless
- awkward pauses
- crowd reactions
- overconfident propaganda sound design
- people continuing their own conversations while absurd things happen nearby

Do not use exaggerated cartoon sound effects as the default humor language.

### 4. Emotional Depth Sits Under the Noise

This world is loud, but it is not shallow.

Under the humor and momentum there should be:

- longing
- memory
- warmth
- fatigue
- neighborhood tenderness
- pride that survives inconvenience

The soundtrack should be able to hold that emotional undertone without turning melodramatic.

## Musical Identity

The score should draw energy from a blend of urban African musical reference points without flattening them into one export-ready sound.

Influence areas can include:

- highlife guitar warmth
- afrobeats momentum
- amapiano spacious groove
- Congolese performance energy
- choir and call-and-response textures
- hand percussion and street percussion logic
- bass-heavy speaker culture
- tizita-like longing in reflective passages

These are influence lanes, not copy targets.

## Score Philosophy

The score should behave in three main modes:

### 1. City Pulse

This is the music of movement, daily rhythm, traffic, errands, gossip, and public life.

Qualities:

- groove-forward
- warm
- percussive
- socially alive

### 2. District Identity

Each district should have its own musical logic, instrumentation emphasis, and crowd rhythm.

This makes movement through the city feel like movement through real social territory.

### 3. Emotional Undercurrent

Quieter cues should carry memory and feeling without becoming sentimental wallpaper.

This is where the game reminds the player that joy existed before the blackout and matters after it.

## District Sound Identity

## Moon Market

**Mood:** warm, dense, local, busy

**Core sounds:**

- bargaining voices
- radios layered over each other
- sewing machines
- frying oil and utensils
- foot traffic
- occasional laughter that turns into gossip

**Musical feel:**

- human-scale percussion
- guitar or plucked textures
- warm bass
- conversational rhythmic phrasing

## Generator Row

**Mood:** mechanical, improvised, high-energy

**Core sounds:**

- welding sparks
- grinders
- engine testing
- power hum
- speaker checks
- metallic impacts

**Musical feel:**

- heavier low-end
- syncopated machine-like percussion
- sharper transients
- rougher texture without losing groove

## Choir Mile

**Mood:** ceremonial, communal, emotionally open

**Core sounds:**

- rehearsal fragments
- choir warmups
- MC mic checks
- dress shoes on tile
- event prep chatter
- amplified prayer or singing in the distance

**Musical feel:**

- vocal stacks
- organ or keys used sparingly
- call-and-response phrasing
- celebratory uplift with emotional weight underneath

## Tideglass

**Mood:** humid, nocturnal, stylish, restless

**Core sounds:**

- waves under city noise
- grills and food stalls
- nightlife bleed from bars or clubs
- port labor texture
- motorcycles and traffic wash
- distant bass through walls

**Musical feel:**

- club-adjacent pulse
- more space in the mix
- reflective melodic lines
- stronger night-energy bass movement

## Civic Crown

**Mood:** polished, controlled, emotionally colder

**Core sounds:**

- cleaner traffic beds
- HVAC and fountain textures
- rehearsed announcements
- security radio chatter
- quieter interiors with artificial air

**Musical feel:**

- more restrained arrangements
- cleaner, more controlled timbres
- less human looseness
- subtle tension under the polished surface

## Monument Square

**Mood:** theatrical, excessive, politically staged

**Core sounds:**

- propaganda jingles
- event countdown language
- oversized sound systems
- generators hidden behind polished structures
- stage crew urgency
- crowd-control announcements

**Musical feel:**

- forced grandeur
- inflated brass or ceremonial textures where appropriate
- official-sounding motifs that can break apart during the climax
- spectacle collapsing into exposed machinery

## The Blackout as Audio Event

The blackout must be felt in the ear, not just seen on screen.

What changes when power is lost:

- music cuts or thins out
- refrigeration, fans, signage, and public systems disappear
- generators become more noticeable
- voices change because people are reacting to missing infrastructure
- silence appears in places that should never be silent

The emotional effect should be:

- wrongness
- irritation
- interruption
- social imbalance

When power returns, audio should help sell relief before dialogue says anything.

## Sound Design Direction

## Electricity

Electricity should have a recognizable sonic identity throughout the game.

It should feel:

- unstable when stolen
- satisfying when restored
- dangerous when weaponized
- infrastructural rather than magical

Avoid making electrical sounds too sci-fi. Keep them grounded in cables, transformers, generators, speakers, and city systems.

## Cloth and Fabric

Because fashion matters so much, cloth should matter sonically too.

Important cloth qualities:

- movement swish for fast traversal
- weight differences between wraps, coats, and light tailoring
- satisfying accent sounds during spins, jumps, and quick turns

These sounds should be subtle but present enough to support identity.

## Tools and Combat

Combat sound should reinforce improvisation.

Player gear should sound:

- crafted
- mechanical
- adaptable
- stylish

Key sound families:

- measuring tape snap and recoil
- hook-spool launch and catch
- reinforced shear strikes
- cable crackle and tension release
- speaker-wave or mic-feedback-inspired disruption effects

## Food and Social Spaces

Food spaces should sound comforting and specific.

Use:

- oil crackle
- metal spoons and cups
- chopping
- serving motions
- low conversation
- nearby radio or music spill

These spaces should sound like care.

## Coffee Ceremony

The coffee ceremony is one of the game's signature audio moments.

Key sounds:

- charcoal heat
- beans or grounds being handled
- pouring into small cups
- ceramic contact
- quieter, closer conversation
- city noise still present, but softened by attention

The ceremony is important because it changes tempo. The player should hear that shift immediately. The city does not disappear; it moves one layer back.

## Radio Direction

Mama Moon's pirate radio is one of the audio anchors of the game.

Radio should provide:

- mission framing
- gossip
- public mood
- satirical commentary
- emotional continuity between districts

### Radio Style

- confident
- dryly funny
- intimate despite broadcast reach
- slightly rough around the edges
- clearly not official

Radio processing should feel handmade and real, not like a generic filter preset dropped on top of clean voice.

## Propaganda Audio

The Honourable's media presence should sound expensive, controlled, and deeply embarrassing.

Key traits:

- overproduced voice-of-authority tone
- triumphant but empty musical beds
- slogans repeated too often
- public-address systems that assume obedience

The joke is not that propaganda sounds incompetent. The joke is that it sounds polished while saying nonsense.

## Voice Direction

Voice acting should prioritize truth of rhythm over flattening everyone into one “neutral” performance style.

Dialogue should feel:

- precise
- lived-in
- socially aware
- rhythmically distinct by character

### Performance Rules

- deadpan is powerful
- fake politeness should land clearly
- crowd reactions should feel layered, not looped
- sincerity matters most when the game gets quiet
- no one should sound like they are explaining the culture to an outsider

## Crowd Audio

Crowds are a character system, not just ambience.

They should communicate:

- rumor spread
- public opinion
- local frustration
- delight in scandal
- ongoing business that does not revolve around the player

Important crowd state:

- unbothered: people continuing their normal rhythm in the middle of chaos

This state is funny, culturally specific, and vital to the game's soul.

## Boss Fight Audio

Boss encounters should feel like public spectacles unraveling.

Audio can help by blending:

- fight rhythm
- crowd response
- PA systems
- propaganda collapse
- live-event tension

Boss fights should sound like image management failing in real time.

## Dynamic Music and Layering

The game will benefit from music systems that can add or remove layers based on context.

Useful transitions:

- ambient district layer to combat pulse
- blackout state to restored-power state
- private conversation to public chaos
- normal city rhythm to boss spectacle

Dynamic changes should feel musical, not like abrupt track switching whenever possible.

## Mix Priorities

The mix should protect:

- dialogue clarity
- groove
- city detail
- major restoration feedback
- comedic timing

Do not bury the world under wall-to-wall score. The city itself is part of the music.

## Audio Guardrails

- No generic “African drums” shortcut.
- No one-size-fits-all world music bed.
- No comedy driven by goofy stock sound effects.
- No over-clean soundscape that erases street life.
- No sterile silence in districts that should feel inhabited.
- No official propaganda audio that sounds weak or accidental.

## Production Guidance

Recommended tools and workflow:

- composition, editing, and voice cleanup in `REAPER`
- early district soundboards before final composition
- radio voice tests early, because tone will shape the whole world
- prototype blackout and restoration transitions before full soundtrack production
- build a reusable library of city textures by district

## Success Test

The audio direction is working if:

- players can tell which district they are in by ear
- the blackout feels like a social wound, not just a visual gimmick
- radio and crowd sound make the city feel opinionated
- quiet scenes carry depth without losing place
- boss fights sound like public embarrassment becoming justice
