# Story Bible

## Project

**Title:** `Who Sent This Honourable?`

**Format:** Stylish 2D African action-comedy

**Status:** Pre-production narrative foundation

## Core Promise

`Who Sent This Honourable?` is a funny, stylish, emotionally honest 2D action game set in a fictional African city where a vain minister steals the city's electricity for his own public spectacle.

The player does not save the world through destiny or chosen-one mythology. They fight back through neighborhood ingenuity, community pride, style, music, public embarrassment, and refusal to accept nonsense from powerful people.

This is a story about ordinary people facing ridiculous authority with intelligence, noise, swagger, and collective will.

## One-Sentence Premise

On the night of the city's biggest festival, a shameless minister diverts public electricity to power his vanity monument, forcing one determined neighborhood crew to cross the city, restore the light district by district, and disgrace him in public.

## Story Identity

This story should feel:

- loud
- funny
- fashion-forward
- politically absurd
- locally grounded
- emotionally sincere underneath the chaos

This is not a story about Africa as abstraction.
It is a story about one city, one blackout, one selfish man, and the many people who are tired of pretending his behavior is normal.

## Narrative Pillars

### 1. Community Over Hero Worship

The main character matters, but they are never the only source of progress. The city's vendors, mechanics, radio hosts, dancers, aunties, children, market women, drivers, artists, and church people all contribute to the fight.

### 2. Public Absurdity As Political Truth

Authority is funny because it is shameless. The minister lies loudly, explains badly, over-dresses for disaster, and treats public theft like a branding exercise. The comedy comes from confidence colliding with obvious nonsense.

### 3. Style Is Storytelling

Clothing, posture, hair, accessories, and public presentation all communicate values, class, ambition, insecurity, and district identity. Looking powerful is part of how characters survive, perform, threaten, seduce, persuade, and resist.

### 4. Humor Must Protect Human Dignity

The story laughs with people who are surviving chaos, not at them for being African. The joke is never that people are local, poor, loud, religious, expressive, dramatic, or communal. The joke is vanity, hypocrisy, ego, fake professionalism, corruption, and theatrical nonsense.

### 5. The City Has a Soul

Every district should feel inhabited by people with taste, memory, routine, gossip, tensions, and pride. Food, noise, transport, music, ceremony, and public speech are part of the narrative fabric.

## Themes

- public power versus private greed
- dignity in neglected neighborhoods
- style as self-respect
- community memory
- performance politics
- mutual aid in the face of failure
- laughter as resistance
- embarrassment as justice

## Tone

The tone moves between:

- affectionate roasting
- fake-polite outrage
- dramatic public speeches
- neighborhood problem-solving
- sincere frustration
- celebratory noise
- emotional honesty when the lights stay off too long

The game should feel comfortable switching from a hilarious radio rant to a quiet scene about spoiled food, canceled celebrations, or people being tired of being treated as disposable.

## Humor Rules

The humor should follow these rules:

1. People are not jokes because they are African.
2. Authority figures can be ridiculous, fragile, performative, and shameless.
3. Roasting should feel affectionate when used within community.
4. Public language should often sound overly formal in obviously absurd situations.
5. When pain appears, the story should not dodge it with empty irony.

## Cultural Guardrails

- No generic "tribal" design language.
- No safari imagery as a shortcut for African identity.
- No flattening many cultures into one costume pack.
- No poverty tourism.
- No outsider gaze explaining basic local life back to local people.
- No making faith, food, accent, or community structures the butt of the joke.

## Protagonist Function

The protagonist is the player's entry point into the city, but their arc is tied to the neighborhoods around them.

They should feel:

- stylish under pressure
- practical
- quick-thinking
- funny without acting like a comedian
- frustrated by nonsense
- deeply loyal to ordinary people

They are not a perfect rebel archetype. They are someone who knows how often power fails, how communities adapt, and how much it costs when leaders turn public life into self-promotion.

## Villain Function

The Honourable is not a dark fantasy tyrant. He is worse in a more familiar way: vain, theatrical, self-important, insulated, and convinced he deserves applause for basic theft.

He should feel:

- hilarious at first glance
- increasingly infuriating over time
- politically plausible inside the fiction
- obsessed with image
- surrounded by people paid to pretend he makes sense

His evil is not mystery. His evil is shamelessness.

## Story Structure

### Opening

The city is preparing for its biggest night of celebration. Shops are busy, speakers are being tested, tailors are making last-minute fixes, roads are crowded, and everybody has an opinion about who is overdressed.

Then the blackout hits.

At first, people assume it is temporary. Then rumors spread. Backup generators fail. Phones die. Sound systems cut out. Food begins to spoil. Weddings, rehearsals, and festival plans collapse. A public statement arrives full of excuses and polished lies.

The neighborhood realizes this is not an accident.

### Middle

The protagonist and their crew move across districts to restore power lines, repair local systems, gather evidence, and strengthen a citywide coalition. Each district reveals a new version of how the blackout harms daily life and how people respond with style, humor, and invention.

The minister's propaganda grows more shameless as the truth becomes harder to hide. Public frustration rises, and the crew's actions become a rumor, then a nuisance, then a threat to his image.

### Escalation

As the crew gets closer to the truth, they discover that the blackout is powering a vanity monument and a staged public event meant to immortalize the Honourable as the face of progress.

This shifts the mission from survival to exposure.

Getting the lights back is no longer enough. The city must see exactly who stole from them, why he did it, and how ridiculous he looks when the performance falls apart.

### Ending

The climax should feel like a social event, a public reckoning, and a boss fight at once. The final confrontation is not only about defeating the Honourable physically. It is about ruining the controlled image he built around himself.

The ideal ending restores power, dignity, and celebration while ensuring the city gets the last laugh.

## District Story Logic

Each district should express:

- what kind of people live and work there
- what the blackout interrupts
- how the district sounds
- what people wear there
- what local food or gathering places matter
- what kind of corruption or neglect is normalized there
- what kind of communal response emerges

District stories must feel cumulative. Restoring one area should ripple into another through rumor, gratitude, trade, music, or shared outrage.

## Emotional Arc

The emotional progression should move like this:

1. excitement
2. confusion
3. annoyance
4. outrage
5. solidarity
6. momentum
7. public defiance
8. cathartic humiliation of power
9. celebration with emotional residue still visible

The game should earn joy by taking disruption seriously.

## Gameplay-Narrative Alignment

The story works best when mechanics reinforce narrative meaning:

- restoring power supports the central conflict directly
- traversal expresses urgency, agility, and city density
- combat reflects improvised neighborhood resistance
- food buffs reinforce culture and place
- radio commentary keeps the city present in the player's ear
- outfit changes support identity, performance, and gameplay style
- boss fights should feel like public spectacles collapsing in real time

## Recurring Motifs

- generators as heartbeat
- fabric as movement and identity
- microphones, speakers, cables, banners, and lights
- rumor moving faster than official information
- public announcements that sound polished but mean nothing
- people dressing well in the middle of disorder
- laughter breaking tension without erasing truth

## Reference Feel

The story should feel like:

- neighborhood epic rather than royal epic
- public satire rather than detached parody
- stylish civic chaos rather than grim collapse
- local confidence rather than outsider explanation

## Success Test

This story is working if players can say:

- "This city feels real."
- "These people are funny because they are alive, not because they are caricatures."
- "The fashion matters."
- "The minister is ridiculous in a way that feels painfully believable."
- "The ending feels like justice and a party."
