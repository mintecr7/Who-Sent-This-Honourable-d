# Gameplay Pillars

## Purpose

This document defines the pre-production gameplay identity of `Who Sent This Honourable?`

The goal is not to list every mechanic the game could contain. The goal is to protect the core play experience so future design choices stay aligned with the game's tone, world, and production scope.

## Core Player Fantasy

The player should feel like:

- a stylish neighborhood problem-solver
- a fast-moving city runner
- a fighter who uses improvisation more than brute force
- someone restoring power, morale, and momentum
- part of a crew and a community, not a lone savior

This is not a power fantasy about becoming untouchable. It is a confidence fantasy about moving through a living city and pushing back against ridiculous authority with skill, style, and help from other people.

## Genre Direction

Working genre definition:

- 2D side-view action game
- platforming and traversal-focused level flow
- light brawler combat
- story-heavy progression
- district-based mission structure
- music and public-event spectacle integrated into key encounters

The game should be readable and expressive rather than mechanically bloated.

## Pillar 1: Expressive Movement

Movement is one of the game's biggest sources of personality.

Traversal should feel:

- agile
- rhythmic
- stylish
- urban
- readable

The player should feel like they are navigating a dense city built by real people, not abstract platforming blocks.

### Movement Priorities

- quick acceleration with readable stopping
- satisfying jumps and aerial control
- environment interaction through cables, poles, awnings, rooftops, signage, and scaffolds
- momentum chains that reward confidence without demanding perfection

### Why It Matters

The city is one of the game's stars. Movement is how the player experiences its verticality, density, and improvisational routes.

## Pillar 2: Stylish Improvised Combat

Combat should feel clever, fast, and expressive rather than militarized.

The player is not a soldier. They fight with crafted tools, environmental awareness, outfit-driven abilities, and neighborhood ingenuity.

### Combat Qualities

- readable at a glance
- stylish without becoming overlong
- grounded in mobility
- built around quick decisions and repositioning
- capable of humor and spectacle

### Combat Inspirations in Spirit

- fight like someone who uses tools, fabric, cables, and speed
- disrupt, embarrass, disable, and outmaneuver
- let enemies look powerful without turning the core combat into grim brutality

### Enemy Logic

Enemies should reflect the city's political and social systems:

- security personnel
- hired enforcers
- event guards
- electrical hazards
- propaganda-heavy authority figures
- district-specific troublemakers or opportunists

Most opponents should feel beatable through skill and momentum, not stat grinding.

## Pillar 3: Power Restoration As Core Loop

Restoring power is the central action fantasy that ties story and mechanics together.

The player should repeatedly:

1. enter a district in crisis
2. understand what the blackout is disrupting
3. navigate local obstacles
4. repair, reroute, or reclaim systems
5. help the community regain function
6. push the broader citywide resistance forward

This loop gives the game its structure and its emotional payoff.

### Restoration Should Affect the World

Power restoration should visibly change:

- lighting
- sound
- crowd behavior
- vendor activity
- music presence
- side quest availability
- neighborhood morale

Players should feel that action has social consequence.

## Pillar 4: Community Is Part of Progression

This game should not be structured around isolated hero upgrades alone.

Progress comes through:

- crew support
- district trust
- rumor networks
- repaired systems
- outfit unlocks
- food access
- public sentiment

The player's journey should feel woven into a larger communal response.

### Design Implication

Side content should not feel disconnected from the main story. Helping a vendor, DJ, cook, mechanic, or local organizer should strengthen the world and support the player's growth.

## Pillar 5: Style Changes Play

Fashion and gameplay should intersect directly.

Outfits or outfit modules can function as:

- build choices
- ability modifiers
- social access signals
- identity expression

### Example Outfit Directions

- mobility-focused set for speed and aerial routes
- disruption-focused set for crowd control or stun chains
- utility-focused set for restoration, hacking, or tool efficiency
- performance-focused set for showier social or combat bonuses

The key idea is not "armor." The key idea is persona.

## Pillar 6: The City Must Stay Present During Play

The game should never feel like a disconnected sequence of levels with a thin city skin over them.

The city remains present through:

- radio commentary
- ambient crowd reactions
- district-specific music
- evolving public rumor
- visible environmental change after missions
- recurring side characters across multiple spaces

The player should feel watched, discussed, and remembered.

### NPC Independence

The city does not pause for the player. NPCs have places to be, people to argue with, and business that has nothing to do with the mission. A vendor is not standing still waiting to be interacted with — she is managing her stall, and the player needs to earn a moment of her attention. An official is not available just because the player arrives — he is doing something else and the player must navigate around his schedule, not the other way around.

This is not obstacle design. This is how the city stays alive. The most memorable NPC interactions should feel like interrupting someone's actual day.

## Pillar 7: Boss Fights Are Public Spectacles

Major encounters should feel like events, not just health bars.

Bosses should blend:

- combat
- performance
- crowd reaction
- environmental manipulation
- social humiliation or exposure

The most important boss fights should feel like the collapse of somebody's image happening in real time.

## Core Game Loop

The high-level loop should be:

1. receive new district problem through crew, rumor, or radio
2. travel into the district and assess the disruption
3. complete traversal, combat, and restoration objectives
4. help locals with optional or required side situations
5. unlock a meaningful visible recovery in that space
6. gain new tools, trust, or outfit options
7. push closer to the minister's broader scheme

This loop supports both narrative structure and production planning.

## District Gameplay Identity

Each district should provide a distinctive mix of:

- traversal geometry
- soundscape
- enemy pressure
- social texture
- restoration problem type
- local side activities

### District Examples

- Moon Market: dense routes, social stakes, introductory systems
- Generator Row: mechanical hazards, repair puzzles, tool upgrades
- Choir Mile: crowd flow, event timing, performance disruption
- Tideglass: nightlife pacing, reflective surfaces, music-heavy movement
- Civic Crown: security systems, surveillance pressure, elite polish masking corruption
- Monument Square: spectacle climax, propaganda architecture, final exposure

## Traversal Design Rules

- routes should feel urban, improvised, and intentional
- alternate paths should reward observation and confidence
- traversal mastery should make players feel stylish, not merely efficient
- level design should support flowing between combat and movement
- the player should often see life happening around or below them

## Combat Design Rules

- combat must not trap the game in one place for too long
- encounters should allow repositioning and movement expression
- enemy counts and attack patterns should preserve readability
- humor and spectacle should coexist with genuine stakes
- improvised tools and environmental interaction should matter

## Interaction Systems

The game works best with a small number of high-identity systems used well.

## Food

Food should function as more than health recovery.

Possible roles:

- healing
- temporary buffs
- district relationship building
- memory or lore delivery
- pre-boss preparation rituals

Food systems should reinforce comfort, care, and cultural specificity.

### The Coffee Ceremony as Mechanic

The coffee ceremony is a specific interaction type: slow, social, multi-round, and resistant to urgency. When the player sits for coffee with a character, the game slows down. This is intentional.

Three rounds happen. Each round can unlock something — information, trust, a memory, a side quest, a piece of lore — but only if the player stays. Leaving early is allowed. Leaving early means missing what the second or third round would have given.

This mechanic exists because not everything in the city should move at the pace of a crisis. Some things require sitting. The coffee ceremony should feel like a genuine alternative rhythm to the action — not a tutorial, not a cutscene, but a choice to slow down that the game rewards honestly.

## Radio

Pirate radio can support:

- mission framing
- rumor updates
- comedic commentary
- side quest discovery
- citywide emotional progression

Radio is a major tool for making the city feel socially connected.

## Outfit System

Outfits should:

- alter gameplay emphasis
- show off character identity
- support build experimentation without requiring heavy RPG complexity

Outfit systems should be easy to understand and fun to swap.

## District Trust

Trust can function as a soft progression layer tied to:

- helping locals
- restoring services
- protecting events
- resolving local conflicts

Higher trust may unlock:

- shortcuts
- discounts
- special recipes
- new side quests
- social backup during public confrontations

## Progression Philosophy

Progression should reward:

- expression
- social investment
- mastery of movement
- creative use of loadouts
- engagement with the city's people

Progression should not rely on:

- heavy loot tables
- endless stat inflation
- grind for grind's sake
- generic crafting clutter

## Narrative Alignment

Gameplay must constantly reinforce the story's message:

- power theft has real local cost
- communities adapt creatively under pressure
- style and dignity matter
- public authority can be challenged
- collective action feels better than solitary dominance

If a mechanic fights those ideas, it should be cut or reworked.

## Emotional Curve of Play

The game should rhythmically alternate between:

- movement flow
- local comedy
- sudden pressure
- problem-solving
- crowd energy
- visible relief after restoration

Players should often feel:

- "this situation is ridiculous"
- "these people are alive and worth helping"
- "I handled that with style"
- "the city is changing because of what we did"
- "these people were fine before I got here and they will be fine after"

## Scope Guardrails

- Do not turn this into a huge open world.
- Do not overbuild RPG systems that drown the tone.
- Do not make combat so technical that personality disappears.
- Do not make traversal so punishing that the city stops feeling joyful.
- Do not bury the social texture under menus and meters.

This project benefits more from strong authored density than huge breadth.

## Early Prototype Priorities

If prototyping starts soon, the first playable should prove:

1. movement feels good in a dense side-view city space
2. cloth/cable-based combat reads clearly
3. restoring power creates a satisfying world-state change
4. dialogue, radio, and environment can sell humor quickly
5. one neighborhood can feel socially alive before content scale increases

## Success Test

The gameplay pillars are working if players can say:

- "Moving through this city feels cool."
- "The combat feels stylish and local to this character."
- "Restoring power actually changes the world."
- "I feel connected to the neighborhood, not just the mission marker."
- "Boss fights feel like public events falling apart."
