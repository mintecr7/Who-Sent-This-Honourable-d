# World / City Guide

## Purpose

This document defines the current working version of the game's world, with a focus on the city where `Who Sent This Honourable?` takes place.

The goal is not to invent a generic pan-African backdrop. The goal is to create one fictional city that feels specific, stylish, contradictory, musical, political, and lived in.

## Working Canon

**City name:** `Kintaba`

**Country:** Fictional anglophone African republic, name to be finalized later

**World scope:** Single-city story with district-by-district progression

## Real-World Influence Blend

Kintaba is fictional, but its energy should be grounded in a clear blend of lived reference points.

Current influence anchors:

- Lagos for density, public chaos, commerce, confidence, and infrastructure pressure
- Accra for coastal warmth, social texture, humor, and neighborhood intimacy
- Kinshasa for fashion bravado, performance energy, music culture, and visual boldness
- Addis Ababa for bureaucratic theater, coffee ceremony culture, deadpan humor, highland formality colliding with street chaos, and the tizita emotional register underneath everything

These influences are directional, not extractive. The city should feel inspired, not copied.

## Core City Identity

Kintaba is a coastal commercial capital where music, politics, tailoring, hustle, ceremony, and public commentary all happen at full volume.

It is a city of:

- traffic and tide
- generators and gossip
- church shoes and football dust
- wedding convoys and roadside smoke
- overbuilt political spectacle and underfunded public systems
- impossible confidence in the middle of inconvenience

This is a place where people dress well for hardship, remember every insult, know which shortcut saves twenty minutes, and can tell from one official statement whether the government is lying badly or lying with effort.

## Story Relevance

Kintaba is not background. The plot only works because this city has:

- visible class contrast between districts
- rich public culture built around electricity, sound, and celebration
- a population that is socially observant and politically talkative
- communities used to improvising when systems fail
- enough noise, rumor, and spectacle for a minister's theft to become a citywide social crisis

## Citywide Tone

Kintaba should feel:

- hot
- stylish
- coastal
- overcrowded in some places and over-curated in others
- deeply social
- emotionally intelligent
- funny in a sharp, local, public way

## Visual Language

The city's visual direction should emphasize:

- bold painted signage
- layered architecture rather than clean futuristic uniformity
- market fabrics, corrugated metal, plaster, tile, banners, lights, and hand-made repairs
- formal clothes in informal spaces
- political branding everywhere it does not belong
- public color used with confidence rather than minimalism

## Sound of the City

Kintaba should sound active even when the player is standing still.

Common sound layers:

- buses braking and calling for passengers
- market bargaining
- radios talking over each other
- generators humming like percussion
- prayer, choir rehearsal, or amplified preaching in the distance
- nearby music leaking from shops and compounds
- metal tools, cooking oil, whistles, announcements, and laughter
- coffee ceremony sounds: charcoal heat, pouring, small cups, quiet conversation happening in the middle of everything else

When the blackout deepens, silence should feel wrong. Missing sound is part of the drama.

## The Blackout's Meaning

Electricity in Kintaba is more than infrastructure.

It powers:

- celebration
- commerce
- music
- refrigeration
- tailoring equipment
- public safety
- nightlife
- community gathering

When the Honourable steals power, he is not just switching off bulbs. He is interrupting memory, work, income, food, performance, ceremony, and social dignity.

## Public Life

Kintaba is socially dense. Very little stays private for long.

Public life runs through:

- market lanes
- roadside food stalls
- tailoring shops
- mechanic yards
- bus stops
- barbershops and salons
- churches, prayer grounds, and rehearsal spaces
- courtyards and compound entrances
- radio call-in culture
- coffee corners: small spaces where people sit, slow down, and share everything — the coffee ceremony is not decoration, it is how the city thinks out loud

People watch, comment, roast, sympathize, speculate, and mobilize quickly.

The coffee ceremony matters specifically because it is the one thing that resists urgency. In a city always in motion, sitting for three rounds of coffee is a social contract. It is where decisions get made, rumors get confirmed, and alliances get built. The blackout interrupting a ceremony in progress is a specific kind of violation.

## How the City Speaks

Dialogue across Kintaba should feel:

- rhythmic
- observant
- expressive
- occasionally fake-formal for comic effect
- capable of switching from roast to sincerity in one beat

Language design guidance:

- Use accessible English for the primary script.
- Let sentence rhythm, code-switching texture, and public phrasing carry locality.
- Avoid overloading dialogue with inauthentic slang dumps.
- Reserve region-specific expressions for intentional character flavor, not wallpaper.

## Fashion Logic

Fashion in Kintaba is a social language.

Clothes communicate:

- neighborhood identity
- ambition
- discipline
- performance energy
- profession
- church or ceremony culture
- generational taste

City fashion should combine:

- tailored structure
- athletic function
- fabric richness
- jewelry and accessory storytelling
- modern streetwear confidence
- ceremonial echoes in everyday silhouettes
- woven border detail used as personal and cultural signature — not costume, not decoration, but a specific design language that marks occasion, identity, and care

## Food Culture

Food should appear as social glue, memory, and survival system, not set dressing.

Food spaces in Kintaba are:

- save points
- rumor hubs
- healing spaces
- side quest anchors
- district identity markers

The food direction can draw from a range of African urban staples and street culture, but each district should have its own local favorites, specialties, and preparation rituals.

## Transport Culture

Movement through Kintaba should reflect:

- packed minibuses
- bike couriers
- roadside congestion
- informal shortcuts
- rooftop improvisation
- drainage channels, alleys, market scaffolds, unfinished structures, and sea-facing edges

Traversal fantasy should feel tied to real urban density rather than abstract platformer geometry.

## District Overview

Kintaba should be divided into distinct districts that feel socially and visually different while remaining part of one ecosystem.

## 1. Moon Market

**Role in game:** Starting district and neighborhood home base

**Identity:** Dense market neighborhood with tailors, food stalls, repair shops, low-rise compounds, and a strong social memory

**Why it matters**

Moon Market is where the player learns what the blackout costs ordinary people. It sets the emotional baseline of the game. This is not the poorest possible district and not the most glamorous. It is simply a deeply alive neighborhood expected to absorb neglect without complaint.

**Visual cues**

- hanging fabrics
- layered awnings
- hand-painted signs
- stacked goods
- exposed wiring
- fashion everywhere

**Sound cues**

- bargaining
- sewing machines
- radios
- frying oil
- children arguing
- gossip with no indoor voice

**Story function**

- establishes community stakes
- introduces Mama Moon, Auntie Sewa, and local allies
- teaches that humor survives side by side with frustration

## 2. Generator Row

**Role in game:** Mechanics and infrastructure district

**Identity:** Workshops, speaker yards, tool markets, welders, electricians, spare parts, illegal power workarounds

**Why it matters**

Generator Row shows the city's unofficial engineering backbone. This is where K1's expertise makes the most sense and where the player sees how much public life already depends on private improvisation.

**Visual cues**

- sparks
- stacked engines
- metal scrap
- cable tangles
- painted shutters
- customized workwear

**Sound cues**

- grinders
- test speakers
- hammering
- revving generators
- arguments over parts and prices

**Story function**

- deepens the power theme mechanically
- reveals how normalized system failure has become
- unlocks restoration tools and city access

## 3. Choir Mile

**Role in game:** Performance, worship, and ceremony district

**Identity:** Churches, rehearsal halls, event rentals, formalwear shops, beauty salons, wedding traffic, poster walls

**Why it matters**

Choir Mile captures the city's emotional and ceremonial life. The blackout here threatens celebrations, rehearsals, and community gatherings that people have planned and paid for carefully.

**Visual cues**

- pressed outfits
- decorated storefronts
- polished shoes
- stage lights waiting for power
- floral and fabric arrangements

**Sound cues**

- choir warmups
- MC rehearsals
- amplified prayer
- event planners shouting
- intermittent sound checks

**Story function**

- gives Pastor Chrome a strong social arena
- shows that power theft attacks joy as much as survival
- supports missions built around public performance and crowd flow

## 4. Tideglass

**Role in game:** Waterfront district of nightlife, trade, and style

**Identity:** Sea breeze, fish grills, bars, night markets, port labor, dance culture, imported goods, luxury trying to imitate local cool

**Why it matters**

Tideglass lets the game breathe into nightlife and music culture. It also shows how the blackout distorts labor and leisure at once, cutting into both entertainment economies and working port rhythms.

**Visual cues**

- reflective water surfaces
- cargo silhouettes
- club signage
- smoke from grills
- wet pavement and bright fabrics

**Sound cues**

- waves under traffic
- dance music bleed
- generators behind bars
- port machinery
- late-night laughter

**Story function**

- expands the music identity of the game
- gives Ayo Flash and DJ Maraba room to shine
- shows class mixing under nightlife conditions

## 5. Civic Crown

**Role in game:** Administrative and elite district

**Identity:** ministry buildings, broad avenues, manicured medians, polished stone, security theater, luxury compounds, image management

**Why it matters**

Civic Crown represents official power and curated visibility. The blackout is least visible here because influence buys backup systems, private solutions, and narrative control. This district must feel smooth, expensive, and emotionally colder than the rest of the city.

**Visual cues**

- government banners
- oversized portraits
- decorative lighting
- tinted SUVs
- marble, glass, and polished gates

**Sound cues**

- cleaner traffic flow
- distant sirens
- rehearsed announcements
- fountain water
- muted HVAC hum in elite interiors

**Story function**

- contrasts sharply with working districts
- reveals propaganda machinery
- sets up the villain's self-image and isolation

## 6. Monument Square

**Role in game:** Final act district and climax location

**Identity:** Event grounds built around the Honourable's vanity project

**Why it matters**

Monument Square is where corruption becomes impossible to hide. It is a monument to misused electricity, political ego, and forced admiration. Everything about it should feel expensive, unnecessary, and faintly embarrassing even before the final confrontation begins.

**Visual cues**

- giant light installations
- branded flags
- over-designed stage architecture
- backup generators hidden in decorative structures
- giant portraiture and slogans

**Sound cues**

- rehearsal speeches
- propaganda jingles
- crowd-control announcements
- overpowered sound systems
- nervous staff coordination

**Story function**

- final reveal of the theft's full scale
- public exposure of Bemba Shine
- climax blending boss fight and civic spectacle

## District Progression Logic

The ideal flow is:

1. Moon Market for personal stakes
2. Generator Row for mechanical empowerment
3. Choir Mile for ceremonial and emotional stakes
4. Tideglass for nightlife, movement, and social reach
5. Civic Crown for political confrontation
6. Monument Square for the public takedown

This order gives the story increasing scale without losing the community core.

## Institutions That Matter

These systems shape everyday life in Kintaba:

- pirate radio and call-in culture
- local councils and ministry offices
- informal repair networks
- food vendors and market associations
- churches and event circuits
- transport unions
- security checkpoints and permit culture

The world should show how official and unofficial systems overlap constantly.

## Propaganda Culture

The Honourable's presence should be felt citywide through:

- banners
- slogans
- radio ads
- oversized portraits
- event posters
- "public service" announcements that feel like campaign material

His branding should feel pushy, overconfident, and slightly desperate.

## Social Humor of the City

Kintaba is funny because people are:

- observant
- blunt
- expressive
- good at spotting hypocrisy
- comfortable turning irritation into theater

Humor often takes the form of:

- fake respect masking obvious criticism
- public commentary that spreads instantly
- over-serious reactions to ridiculous events
- elite language reused mockingly by ordinary people
- bureaucratic deadpan: officials and clerks who treat the people waiting for them as an inconvenience, operating on their own logic with complete unbothered confidence, never explaining, never apologizing, fully convinced the inconvenience runs the other direction
- the joke is never that authority is cruel — the joke is that authority is completely sincere about its own importance

## Emotional Undertone

Underneath the noise and comedy, Kintaba carries a tizita quality — a sense of depth and longing that is not sadness but is not pure lightness either. People here have memory. The city has survived things. The humor and warmth are not performed resilience. They are what people actually feel.

This register should surface in quieter moments: a vendor who has kept the same stall for thirty years, a song playing from somewhere unseen, a ceremony that continues despite everything. The game earns its emotional peaks because this undertone is always present, never announced.

## Success Test

The city guide is working if Kintaba feels like:

- a place with its own memory
- a place where style is survival and expression
- a place where electricity changes emotional weather
- a place where politics is public performance
- a place worth saving because people already love living there
- a place where happiness is the starting condition, not the reward
